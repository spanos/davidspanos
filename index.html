"Most likely to win national poetry slam with a piece written in Python"

Watch enough performances on TED or YouTube and you'll soon notice the unmistakable cadence that characterizes slam poetry. A mix of gestures, varied tempo, and emphatic pauses, it's a powerful technique. Maybe too powerful. What started as a vehicle for the delivery of raw emotion has become a trope, and like all things popular, a target for satire. This realization hit me during a guest session of slam poetry at Di Phi, UNC's speech and debate society. Later that night, I grabbed a Python file from the Bitcoin repository on GitHub and recited its contents with conviction, slam poetry-style. My Di Phi superlative probably won't come true, but I'm glad to have earned it with laughter.

Though an engineer by training and a recent entrant to the world of hackathons and startups, I'm first and foremost a reader, writer, talker, and thinker. Technology is advancing at a rate unprecedented in human history. Fun and rewarding as it is to design, program, and solder new technologies, these advances are inevitable. Much less certain is how we use them. I want to position businesses to nurture mankind's ever more intimate connection with technology in a way that benefits society as much as shareholders and employees.
